# Canadian Triage and Acuity Scale (CTAS) Guidelines

The CTAS scale maps patient condition to time-to-treatment. In our system, CTAS levels map to Urgency Levels:
CTAS Level 1 (Resuscitation) -> URGENCY: RED
CTAS Level 2 (Emergent) -> URGENCY: RED
CTAS Level 3 (Urgent) -> URGENCY: YELLOW
CTAS Level 4 (Less Urgent) -> URGENCY: GREEN
CTAS Level 5 (Non-Urgent) -> URGENCY: GREEN

## CTAS LEVEL 1 (Resuscitation / RED)
Immediate life-saving intervention required. Time to physician: IMMEDIATE.
- Cardiac or Respiratory Arrest.
- Major Trauma (shock, GCS < 9, severe respiratory distress).
- Shortness of breath (Severe): SpO2 < 90% on O2, extreme work of breathing.
- Level of consciousness: Unconscious (GCS 3-9).

## CTAS LEVEL 2 (Emergent / RED)
Conditions that are a potential threat to life, limb or function. Time to physician: <= 15 minutes.
- Head Injury: GCS 10-13.
- Severe pain (Pain scale 8-10): e.g., suspected kidney stone pain, acute sickle cell crisis.
- Chest pain (Cardiac features): Suspected ischemia, tearing chest/back pain (aortic dissection).
- Shortness of breath (Moderate): SpO2 < 92% on room air.
- Temperature: > 38.0°C in immunocompromised or HR > 130.

## CTAS LEVEL 3 (Urgent / YELLOW)
Conditions that could potentially progress to a serious problem. Time to physician: <= 30 minutes.
- Head Injury: GCS 14-15 with normal vitals but some nausea/vomiting.
- Moderate pain (Pain scale 4-7).
- Shortness of breath (Mild): SpO2 92-94%, normal work of breathing.
- Mild asthma exacerbation.
- Undifferentiated abdominal pain with normal vitals.

## CTAS LEVEL 4/5 (Less/Non-Urgent / GREEN)
Conditions related to patient age, distress, or potential for deterioration or complications would benefit from intervention or reassurance within 1-2 hours.
- Minor trauma (sprains, minor lacerations).
- Chronic back pain.
- Uncomplicated upper respiratory infection (URI), SpO2 > 95%.
- Pain scale 1-3.
